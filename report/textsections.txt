Abstract----

Humans are able to navigate and understand new environments quickly and intuitively,
combining sensory inputs with spatial memory to create an understanding their environment
and their location with it. Replicating this in machines is a complicated task encompassing
various areas of research and is a key challenge in developping autonomous robots and vehicles.
Simulatenous Localization and Mapping (SLAM) is a class of algorithms dedicated to just this task, 
allowing for systems to understand the environment in which they are, and where they are in it -especially critical 
in scenarios where outside sources of information, such as GPS, are unavailable. 
This project presents a C++ implementation of a Graph-Based SLAM backend developed from first principles. 
The resulting system demonstrates reliable convergence in pose-graph optimization, validating that a ground-up implementation can manage the underlying mathematical complexity and high-performance requirements of modern robotics

--Motivation 
<P1: Why SLAM is needed>
Robotic Systems sense the world and themselves through a variety of sensors, such as wheel encoders, gyroscopes, and lidar sensors. The two former are combined to 
create odometry data, estimating the change in position and orientation (Pose), and the latter using laser reflections
to sense the geometry of the world at a distance. In real world settings, odometry data carries noise from sensor readings and misalignments in these readings to 
the actions they imply : for example, wheels slipping and bumps on road. This causes drift - an error in the calculated pose compared to the actual pose. As these pose estimates
build on top of eachother, this drift quickly accumulates and leads to worsening estimates, and new sensor inputs conflict with these internal estimates, leading to catastrophic
errors in localization and mapping. To illustrate the effects of an erroneous pose estimate, lidar data might say that there is a wall 3m direcly ahead, but out pose estimates estimates that we are at (3m, 1m) while in reality 
we are at (2,0) - we now believe that there is a wall at (6,1) while in reality it's at (5,0).
A Graph Based SLAM system makes a graph of poses and movements, and when it detects a loop closure - a place where it's been before - it uses this new datapoint at this repeated location to 
update all it's previous poses and estimates of the world around, to "snap" it's trajectory and mapping into a corrected version.


<P2: Why I chose to build it>

<P1: Explanation of SLAM>
A SLAM system starts with sensor inputs, and over time accumulates information to locate itself and map it's surroundings. 
It is composed of a chain of subsystems, each varying complexities, points of failure, and depend on the step before it to produce meaningful results. 
This loop repeats at each timestep, building on top of the information that was calculated by the last "round" 

Simple Overview:
We use our sensors to estimate our movement in the world. As we move along, we build our graph that tracks "keyframes" : where we were and how the world looked. 
Once we make a loop and we notice "hey, I've been here before", we pass our graph through an optimizer which snaps everything into place, reducing the error and uncertainty
that we accumulate as we go around the world with our unperfect sensors.

<b>Issues with Rotation <b> 
ICP: 
Rotations make point clouds change drastically from little movement, so ICP by it's nature struggle to find good transformations when undergoing through large rotations. 
Thus, we do some <b> gating <b>, where if we are rotating fast enough, we use the transformation estimated by odometry rather than running ICP. 
This is a simple and effective fix we used, but could be improved. In a production SLAM system, better sensor fusion would be used, such as an <b> Extended Kalman Filter <b> 
to intelligently use various sensor's informations when one cannot be fully relied on

Webots: 
Even after introducing rotation gating for localization, we were still having big issues with map smear. 
After countless hours of troubleshooting, I noticed with was an issue with Webots. While rotating, the lidar scan twitches back and forth, until finally once rotation is finished, 
it snaps back into place. This means, we are getting very noisy and incorrect lidar data. 
Thus, we only use lidar data when we are not rotating, when it is guaranteed to be correct.

4) Keyframes and building the Graph
Here we start building our graph, our record of where we've been and how the world has looked at each of these timesteps. 
We add keyframes only once in a while, once we've moved some X distance, or rotated some Y degrees. 
These are parameters we tune from trial and error

Graph Nodes: Contain
        * <b>Pose<b>: (estimated) location and angle
        *Point cloud: how the world looked
        * ID and Timestamp (for identification purposes)
      

Edges: Given this new Node, the transform between our last node and now. How we must have moved to get here
      * Information: how good is this measurement? We use the difference between the prediction from odometry vs ICP to measure this

5) Loop Closure
As we traverse the world and add keyframes, we compare our current position with old graph nodes to see if we've been here before. 
Note that "here" isn't exact, we can be some distance away, just have to be close enough.
If we have, we run ICP with old nodes and get the best match. This tells us if we can confidently say that we've made a full loop. 
We add a new node to our graph, representing the current location, and then a special <b> loop closure edge<b> between this node, and the old node that we had a match with. 
**add picture** 

If we are unable to add a loop closure edge due to not finding any good matches, or ICP not converging, we continue like normal 



6) Graph Optimization 
Now that we've added a loop closure edge, we've made a full loop and are ready to optimize our graph.

What is optimizing our graph? We find the arrangements of nodes that minimize the error of the graph that we pass into our optimizer. 
Let's define this rigorously

Let x be our state, our nodes. 
Let Zi^ = real observations, the edges in our graph which is the movement between nodes as predicted by ICP
Let Zi = predicted observation, which is simply taking the differnce in location from the stored position of our nodes   ** Add picture **

We define our error e_i(x) = Zi - Zi^ :  the difference between our real and predicted robot movements
We don't want a notion of positive vs negative error, only error, so we will get the squared error. 

We will assume this error is normally distributed with mean 0. 
We define our information matrix Omega. This is the weight of each measurement - how confident are we in this? 
    - Here we use the information from our edge : how different our ICP measurement is vs odometry

Then our weighted sum of squared error , ei(x) = ei(x)Omega*ei(x)

Then, we want to find nodes X that minimize the sum of this squared error

    Goal: X = argmin(x) sigma(ei(x)*Omega*ei(x))


This is our system that we pass into our optimizer. 

We use GTSAM, a well known and well tested implementation pose graph optimizer. 

We use Levenberg-Marquadt optimizer which is a popular implementation of nonlinear least squares.

If our pose graph optimizer was able to find a good solution, below some error threshold, we update our pose graph and reproject our lidar readings to these new poses.
